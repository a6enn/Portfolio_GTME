The Buyer

Founder-led brands without a finance team.

Highbeam consolidates banking, cards, capital, bill pay, and AI finance agents into one platform. Sweet spot: bootstrapped consumer brands at $5M–$30M revenue, no dedicated finance function. The founder is running cash from a spreadsheet and a gut feeling — until they hit the wall. That moment is what the signal stack was built to catch.

The Frame

Fit tells you who could buy. Timing tells you who's ready.

Most prospect lists answer the wrong question. They tell you a company could be a customer. The valuable question — and the one that closes deals — is whether the founder has hit the wall yet. That moment has a behavioral signature. It shows up in hiring, in spend, in tooling — before it ever shows up in a sales conversation.

Fit is the TAM. Timing is the wedge inside it that's actually ready to move.
A great prospect ready to move is every company's dream. Fit-only lists never tell you that.
The Three Signals

Behavior beats demographics.

Each signal had to be time-bound and specific — sharp enough that it couldn't be explained by anything unrelated to financial tooling. Any two firing together push a company into Tier 1.

Inside the Build

One workbook. Three layers. Three signals.

Source list, person enrichment, scored output — three tables stacked inside a single Clay workbook. The Custom Table is where the three signals get checked, weighted, and rolled up into a tier. Every row is a brand sitting on a live decision window or one signal away from it.

Stylized view of the live workbook (April 2026). Real brand archetypes, real columns, real tier logic.

The signal columns aren't a separate model. They are the table — every row is scored the moment its source data lands, and every tier reshuffles as new signals arrive. The list is a live ranking, not a static export.

What Got Cut

Most signals look useful until you ask what they're really telling you.

Fundraising activity, LinkedIn follower growth, G2 review behavior, social engagement. None made the cut. Follower growth tells you a brand is building an audience. G2 reviews tell you a decision was made months ago. Neither tells you a founder is sitting on a cash problem they're ready to solve this quarter. The bar was specificity to financial tooling — anything that could be explained by something else got cut.

The Next Layer

Snapshot today, monitor forever.

The version I built is a snapshot — three independent signals, scored once. The architecture worth running in production is continuous: every change on a monitored account fires a webhook, drops a flag into Salesforce, creates a task. The system stops telling you who could be a customer. It starts telling you that an account you already know just crossed into a live decision window — and today is the day to call.

The next leverage point is sequence. A founder visits Highbeam's website, then weeks later a Head of Finance role goes up. That sequence is sharper than either signal alone — the visit wasn't random, the founder was already thinking about infrastructure before they decided to hire.
