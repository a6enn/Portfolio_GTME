The Positioning Bet

Finance is running an outbound motion. It just calls it deal sourcing.

A deal-sourcing analyst at a middle-market PE firm runs the same machine as a BDR. Build a target universe. Enrich. Score by fit. Reach the right contact first. The output is different — a company to acquire, not a customer to close — but the motion is identical. Most analysts run that machine on PitchBook exports, Affinity, and manual research that should not be manual.

Same four steps. Different output. The tools haven't caught up.
Proprietary deal flow is the entire game. Sourcing earlier than competitors is alpha — and it compounds.

Funds that win consistently aren't relying on partner networks. They're competing on infrastructure. The clearest organizational tell that a firm has made that bet is a dedicated Business Development or Originations hire — sourcing as a process, not a relationship game.

The Cut

Seven disqualifiers, applied in order.

Financial services runs from JPMorgan down to a solo GP managing $50M. Most of it isn't a fit. The discipline of an ICP isn't who it includes — it's everything you delete on the way to the right list.

Too large to need it
Household-name firms win on reputation. Outbound sourcing matters most to firms still earning that position.
Publicly traded
Banks, asset managers, brokerages don't run outbound deal sourcing — or solved it internally years ago.
Wrong investment type
Hedge funds, mutual funds, wealth managers, family offices aren't hunting private companies to back.
No active fund
A firm in harvest mode is managing positions. Deployment pressure is what creates urgency.
No sourcing headcount
No dedicated BD or Originations role means the firm hasn't made the organizational bet on systematic sourcing.
Wrong sourcing motion
Fund-of-funds, secondaries, CLO managers don't prospect companies directly. Different motion entirely.

A seventh, lighter cut runs at the end: systematic or quant strategies. If a firm has already built its data infrastructure, Clay doesn't add a layer. Claygent flags those from each firm's own website copy.

The Data Hack

Bloomberg, PitchBook, and S&P locked the door. Regulation forced one open.

Clay's native enrichments are built for SaaS. Financial services is a different problem: the incumbents spent decades building proprietary databases of private fund data — that data is their product, expensive and deliberately unavailable through the third-party APIs Clay integrates with. Trying to build a fin-services TAM the way you'd build a SaaS TAM, you run out of road fast.

Every private fund raise triggers a Form D filing. The SEC publishes them. Clay's HTTP column reads them.

Two things broke and had to be designed around. EDGAR registrant names are legal entities, not brands — Andreessen Horowitz returns 900 hits because every fund vehicle files separately. The count is noisy, but the existence of a recent filing is reliable, which is all the disqualifier needed. The API also doesn't sort by date, so the plan to extract the most recent filing simplified into a binary pass/fail.

A good GTM engineer can build a flow. A great one knows where the information lives — and where regulation forced the door open anyway.
The Three Segments

Real segmentation cuts by motion, not by filter.

The qualified universe came in at 454 firms. Three motions emerged — each with its own messaging logic, sourcing pain, and playbook.

Each segment got a 150-word playbook, a different status-quo tool to position Clay against, and a different sector emphasis. A single message to all 454 firms is louder than the audience. Three messages routed by motion is the difference between a list and a system.

The Email Engine

63 unique combinations. Zero AI credits.

Each segment splits further by investment vehicle, sector, and seniority — and each cut changes the message. Hand-writing every variation is impractical. Burning Claygent credits for it is wasteful when the structure is deterministic. So the engine runs on five formula columns composing every message from three inputs: segment, sector, seniority.

COMMENT_1
Problem statement by title
COMMENT_2
Sector-specific proof line
CONNECTOR
Positions Clay vs. status quo
CTA
Closes with firm name
FINAL_EMAIL
Assembles all four

A Partner gets a different opening argument than an Analyst. "Every founder-led SaaS company in your ARR band, enriched with hiring velocity" is a different sentence than "every family-owned industrial that never shows up in PitchBook." Five formulas, three inputs, 63 deterministic combinations. Fully editable. Zero credits.

For delivery, the table feeds a Smartlead sequence — one contact status column tracked by webhook from Ready to Send through Pushed to CRM — with channel choice routed by segment. Equity Acquirers run email; Venture Hunters run LinkedIn, where VCs build public presence. Capital Originators follow the same email path as PE. The deterministic engine sits where Claygent would have, doing $0.05/contact AI work at the cost of a formula recalc.

The AI Layer, Earned

Trim before AI runs.

Formulas handle structure. They can't say anything specifically true about the person receiving the email — that's Claygent's job, and Claygent costs credits. Running it on 190 contacts wastes most of them on people who wouldn't pass a basic filter. Trim aggressively before AI runs at all.

A final Claygent screen kept only the records where the summary was actually about the individual — not the firm or the fund strategy. Five prospects. Verified emails, relevant titles, summaries personal enough to write a sentence against. The honest list — built without burning credits on anyone who wouldn't have made it past basic filtering.

The Discipline

The infrastructure was real. The decision to send was a separate question.

Five contacts cleared every filter. The machine was complete. Before any send, the question that actually matters is the simplest one. Have I earned the right to make this claim, to this person, in this market?

Sending without that ground is not GTM engineering. It's hypothesis laundering dressed up as outreach.

Reaching senior PE professionals with a representation that hasn't been validated — without coordinating with Clay's existing financial-services presence — is a different action than the engineering. It's a claim, and a claim has to be earned. Discovery first. Reference case. Then the send.

That's the pipeline this build actually produced — and it's worth more than a send rate.
